Introduction to Pharmacy for Technicians

Welcome to your essential resource for mastering the core knowledge required for a successful career as a Pharmacy Technician. This hub is designed to provide clear, concise, and accessible information on key topics, from understanding medications to navigating complex calculations and abiding by critical laws and regulations.

A Pharmacy Technician plays a vital role in healthcare, working closely with pharmacists to ensure patients receive the correct medications safely and efficiently. Your responsibilities can range from preparing prescriptions and managing inventory to assisting with insurance and patient interactions. This platform aims to support your learning journey and help you stay current in this dynamic field.

Drug Calculations

Mastering pharmacy calculations is crucial for accuracy and patient safety. Practice these common types of problems:

1. Days' Supply Practice Problems

  • Problem: A patient's prescription is for **Insulin Glargine (Lantus)** 100 units/mL. They are instructed to inject 20 units subcutaneously every day. The vial size is 10 mL. What is the days' supply?
    Solution:
    Total units in vial = 10 mL vial × 100 units/mL = 1,000 units.
    Daily dose = 20 units/day.
    Days' supply = 1,000 units / 20 units/day = 50 days.
  • Problem: A patient is prescribed **Amoxicillin 250mg capsules** for 14 days. The instructions are to take 1 capsule three times a day (TID). How many capsules should be dispensed?
    Solution:
    Doses per day = 3 doses/day
    Total doses = 3 doses/day × 14 days = 42 capsules.
    Total quantity to dispense = 42 capsules.
  • Problem: A prescription for **Liquid Prednisolone** is 15mg once daily. The concentration is 5mg/mL, and the total volume dispensed is 60mL. What is the days' supply?
    Solution:
    Dose per day in mL = 15mg / 5mg/mL = 3mL/day.
    Days' supply = 60mL / 3mL/day = 20 days.

2. Clark's Rule (Pediatric Dosage by Weight)

  • Formula: (Patient's weight in lbs / 150 lbs) × Adult Dose = Pediatric Dose. Problem: A 75-lb child needs **Amoxicillin**. The adult dose is 500 mg. What is the correct pediatric dose using Clark's Rule?
    Solution:
    (75 lbs / 150 lbs) × 500 mg = 0.5 × 500 mg = 250 mg.
  • Problem: An adult dose of a medication is 200 mg. Calculate the dose for a 50-lb child using Clark's Rule.
    Solution:
    (50 lbs / 150 lbs) × 200 mg = 0.333... × 200 mg ≈ 66.67 mg.
  • Problem: A 30-lb child needs a medication. The adult dose is 150 mg. Using Clark's Rule, what is the pediatric dose?
    Solution:
    (30 lbs / 150 lbs) × 150 mg = 0.2 × 150 mg = 30 mg.

3. Young's Rule (Pediatric Dosage by Age)

  • Formula: (Age in years / (Age in years + 12)) × Adult Dose = Pediatric Dose. Problem: A 6-year-old child needs a medication with an adult dose of 10 mg. What is the correct pediatric dose using Young's Rule?
    Solution:
    (6 / (6 + 12)) × 10 mg = (6 / 18) × 10 mg = 0.333 × 10 mg ≈ 3.33 mg.
  • Problem: An adult dose is 50 mg. Calculate the dose for a 4-year-old child using Young's Rule.
    Solution:
    (4 / (4 + 12)) × 50 mg = (4 / 16) × 50 mg = 0.25 × 50 mg = 12.5 mg.
  • Problem: An 8-year-old child needs an adult dose of 100 mg. Calculate the pediatric dose using Young's Rule.
    Solution:
    (8 / (8 + 12)) × 100 mg = (8 / 20) × 100 mg = 0.4 × 100 mg = 40 mg.

4. Alligations (Mixing Concentrations)

  • Problem: You need to prepare 60 mL of 50% alcohol solution from a 90% solution and a 20% solution. How much of each is needed?
    Solution:
    1. Place desired concentration (50%) in center.
    2. Place higher (90%) and lower (20%) concentrations at top-left and bottom-left.
    3. Subtract diagonally: (50 - 20) = 30 parts of 90%; (90 - 50) = 40 parts of 20%.
    4. Total parts = 30 + 40 = 70 parts.
    5. Calculate amount of each:
    90% solution: (30 parts / 70 total parts) × 60 mL ≈ 25.71 mL.
    20% solution: (40 parts / 70 total parts) × 60 mL ≈ 34.29 mL.
  • Problem: A pharmacy needs to prepare 100 mL of a 30% dextrose solution using a 70% dextrose solution and a 5% dextrose solution. How much of the 70% solution and 5% solution is required?
    Solution:
    Desired concentration = 30%
    Higher concentration = 70%
    Lower concentration = 5%

    Parts of 70% solution = 30 - 5 = 25 parts
    Parts of 5% solution = 70 - 30 = 40 parts
    Total parts = 25 + 40 = 65 parts

    Amount of 70% solution: (25 / 65) × 100 mL ≈ 38.46 mL
    Amount of 5% solution: (40 / 65) × 100 mL ≈ 61.54 mL
  • Problem: How much 95% alcohol and 45% alcohol are needed to prepare 500 mL of 60% alcohol solution?
    Solution:
    Desired concentration = 60%
    Higher concentration = 95%
    Lower concentration = 45%

    Parts of 95% solution = 60 - 45 = 15 parts
    Parts of 45% solution = 95 - 60 = 35 parts
    Total parts = 15 + 35 = 50 parts

    Amount of 95% solution: (15 / 50) × 500 mL = 150 mL
    Amount of 45% solution: (35 / 50) × 500 mL = 350 mL

5. Flow Rate (IV Administration)

  • Formula: (Volume in mL / Time in hours) = Flow Rate in mL/hr. Problem: A patient needs a 1,000 mL IV bag infused over 8 hours. What is the flow rate?
    Solution:
    1,000 mL / 8 hours = 125 mL/hr.
  • Problem: An IV infusion of 500 mL is to be given over 4 hours. The drop factor of the IV tubing is 20 drops/mL. Calculate the flow rate in drops per minute (gtts/min).
    Solution:
    Volume in mL = 500 mL
    Time in minutes = 4 hours × 60 minutes/hour = 240 minutes
    Drop factor = 20 gtts/mL

    Flow rate (gtts/min) = (Volume in mL × Drop factor) / Time in minutes
    Flow rate = (500 mL × 20 gtts/mL) / 240 minutes = 10000 / 240 ≈ 41.67 gtts/min.
  • Problem: A 250 mL IV is to infuse over 30 minutes. What is the flow rate in mL/hr?
    Solution:
    Time in hours = 30 minutes / 60 minutes/hour = 0.5 hours
    Flow rate = 250 mL / 0.5 hours = 500 mL/hr.

6. Concentrations and Dilutions (C1V1 = C2V2)

  • Formula: C1V1 = C2V2 (Concentration 1 × Volume 1 = Concentration 2 × Volume 2). Problem: A pharmacy has a 500 mL stock solution of a 10% concentration. You need to dilute it to a 2% concentration. What is the final volume?
    Solution:
    (10%) × (500 mL) = (2%) × V2
    5000 = 2 × V2
    V2 = 5000 / 2 = 2500 mL.
  • Problem: You have a 20% stock solution and need to prepare 250 mL of a 5% solution. How much of the 20% stock solution is needed?
    Solution:
    C1 = 20%, V1 = ?
    C2 = 5%, V2 = 250 mL

    C1V1 = C2V2
    (20%) × V1 = (5%) × 250 mL
    20 × V1 = 1250
    V1 = 1250 / 20 = 62.5 mL.
  • Problem: You need to prepare 1 liter of a 0.9% Sodium Chloride (normal saline) solution from a 10% Sodium Chloride stock solution. How much of the 10% stock solution is needed?
    Solution:
    Convert 1 liter to mL: 1 L = 1000 mL
    C1 = 10%, V1 = ?
    C2 = 0.9%, V2 = 1000 mL

    C1V1 = C2V2
    (10%) × V1 = (0.9%) × 1000 mL
    10 × V1 = 900
    V1 = 900 / 10 = 90 mL.

7. Business Math (Profit)

  • Problem: A medication costs the pharmacy $15.50. The patient pays a $10 co-pay, and the insurance company pays $8.25. What is the profit for the pharmacy?
    Solution:
    Total Revenue = $10.00 (co-pay) + $8.25 (insurance) = $18.25.
    Profit = $18.25 (Total Revenue) - $15.50 (Cost) = $2.75.
  • Problem: A prescription item costs the pharmacy $25.00. The third-party payer reimburses $32.00. What is the gross profit for the pharmacy?
    Solution:
    Gross Profit = Reimbursement - Cost
    Gross Profit = $32.00 - $25.00 = $7.00.
  • Problem: A pharmacy purchases 100 tablets of a drug for $50.00. They sell each tablet for $1.50. What is the total profit if all tablets are sold?
    Solution:
    Total Revenue = 100 tablets × $1.50/tablet = $150.00
    Total Cost = $50.00
    Profit = Total Revenue - Total Cost = $150.00 - $50.00 = $100.00.

Medical Terminology and Abbreviations

Understanding medical language is fundamental to working in a pharmacy. Here are some key terms and common abbreviations you'll encounter.

Basic Medical Terminology

  • Pharmacology: The study of drugs and their effects on living systems.
  • Pharmacokinetics: The study of how a drug moves through the body (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion).
  • Adverse Reaction: An undesired or unexpected side effect of a medication.
  • Analgesic: A medication used to relieve pain (e.g., ibuprofen).
  • Antipyretic:: A medication used to reduce fever (e.g., acetaminophen).
  • Contraindication: A specific situation or condition where a particular drug or treatment should not be used because it may be harmful.
  • Deductible: The amount an insured person must pay out of pocket before their insurance company starts to pay.
  • Co-payment (Co-pay): A fixed amount paid by an insured person for a covered healthcare service or prescription.

Medical Term Word Parts (Basic)

  • Cardio-: Pertaining to the heart. (e.g., Cardiology)
  • Hepato-: Pertaining to the liver. (e.g., Hepatitis)
  • Nephro-: Pertaining to the kidneys. (e.g., Nephrology)
  • Pulmo-: Pertaining to the lungs. (e.g., Pulmonary)
  • Gastro-: Pertaining to the stomach. (e.g., Gastroenterology)
  • Neuro-: Pertaining to nerves/nervous system. (e.g., Neurology)
  • Osteo-: Pertaining to bones. (e.g., Osteoporosis)
  • -itis: Inflammation of. (e.g., Arthritis, Bronchitis)
  • -ectomy: Surgical removal of. (e.g., Appendectomy)
  • -pathy: Disease of. (e.g., Neuropathy)
  • -algia: Pain. (e.g., Myalgia - muscle pain)
  • -emia: Blood condition. (e.g., Anemia)
  • anti-: Against. (e.g., Antibiotic, Antihistamine)
  • hyper-: Above, excessive. (e.g., Hypertension)

Laws and Regulations of the Pharmacy

Pharmacy technicians must have a working knowledge of the laws and regulations governing the practice of pharmacy. Adherence to these laws ensures patient safety and legal compliance.

  • HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)

    Summary: This federal law enacted in 1996 primarily aims to protect the privacy and security of patient health information. It establishes national standards for the security of electronic protected health information (ePHI) and sets forth rules regarding its use and disclosure. Pharmacy technicians must be familiar with HIPAA to ensure patient confidentiality and handle protected health information (PHI) appropriately.

    Resource: HIPAA Basics for Healthcare Professionals

    For more information on this subject, search for "HIPAA Basics for Healthcare Professionals" on YouTube or a similar video platform.

  • DEA (Drug Enforcement Administration) Regulations

    Summary: The DEA enforces the Controlled Substances Act (CSA), which regulates the manufacture, distribution, and dispensing of controlled substances. Technicians need to understand the five schedules of controlled substances (C-I, C-II, C-III, C-IV, C-V) and the stringent requirements for their ordering, storage, inventory, dispensing, and record-keeping to prevent diversion and abuse.

    Resource: Controlled Substance Schedules Explained

    For more information on this subject, search for "Controlled Substance Schedules Explained" on YouTube or a similar video platform.

  • FD&C Act (Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act)

    Summary: This act, originally passed in 1938, gives the FDA authority to oversee the safety of food, drugs, medical devices, and cosmetics. It mandates that new drugs must be proven safe and effective before they can be marketed. Key amendments like the Durham-Humphrey Amendment (1951) established the distinction between prescription and over-the-counter drugs, and the Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962) strengthened drug approval requirements, mandating proof of efficacy.

  • Poison Prevention Packaging Act (PPPA)

    Summary: Enacted in 1970, the PPPA requires certain household products and medications to be packaged in child-resistant containers to prevent accidental poisoning in children. Pharmacies must dispense most prescription drugs in such containers unless the patient specifically requests an easy-open cap or the drug is explicitly exempt by law (e.g., nitroglycerin).

  • Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (OBRA '90)

    Summary: OBRA '90 mandated that pharmacists offer to counsel Medicaid patients about their prescriptions. While primarily for pharmacists, technicians often play a role in facilitating this process and understanding its importance in patient care.

  • State Boards of Pharmacy Regulations

    Summary: Each state has its own Board of Pharmacy that governs the practice of pharmacy within that state. These boards regulate pharmacist and technician licensure, continuing education requirements, ratios of technicians to pharmacists, and the specific scope of practice for technicians. Technicians must always be aware of and adhere to their specific state's regulations, as they can vary significantly from federal laws and other states.

Technician Roles & Responsibilities

Pharmacy technicians are integral members of the healthcare team. Their roles can vary slightly depending on the setting, but generally include a core set of responsibilities:

  • Dispensing Medications: Assisting pharmacists in preparing and labeling prescription medications, including counting, pouring, mixing, and measuring.
  • Compounding: Preparing customized medications for patients, which can involve non-sterile compounding (e.g., creams, oral solutions) or sterile compounding (e.g., IV admixtures).
  • Inventory Management: Ordering, receiving, stocking, and organizing medications and supplies, as well as managing expired or recalled products.
  • Patient Information Management: Collecting and updating patient demographics, insurance information, and medication histories.
  • Insurance Processing: Assisting patients with billing and insurance claims, resolving rejections, and understanding co-pays and deductibles.
  • Communication: Interacting with patients (under pharmacist supervision), answering phones, and communicating with healthcare providers and insurance companies.
  • Automated Dispensing Systems: Loading and maintaining medications in automated dispensing cabinets (e.g., Pyxis, Omnicell) in hospital settings.
  • Maintaining Records: Accurately documenting all dispensing activities, controlled substance logs, and patient profiles.
  • Compliance: Adhering to all federal and state laws, regulations, and pharmacy policies, including HIPAA for patient privacy.

Pharmacology Basics

Understanding how drugs interact with the body is fundamental. Here are some core concepts:

Pharmacokinetics: ADME

Pharmacokinetics describes how the body affects a drug. It involves four main processes:

  • Absorption: How the drug moves from its administration site into the bloodstream. Factors like route of administration, drug formulation, and patient's physiological state influence this.
    Example: An oral tablet (like Ibuprofen) must dissolve in the stomach/intestines and be absorbed into the blood. An IV injection (like Insulin administered intravenously) is absorbed almost immediately because it bypasses the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Distribution: How the drug spreads from the bloodstream to various tissues and organs. This depends on blood flow, tissue binding, and how easily the drug can cross biological barriers (e.g., blood-brain barrier).
    Example: A highly protein-bound drug (like Warfarin) has less "free" drug available to act on tissues, as much of it is temporarily bound to plasma proteins.
  • Metabolism (Biotransformation): The process by which the body chemically alters drugs, typically in the liver, to make them easier to excrete. This often involves enzymes, particularly the cytochrome P450 (CYP450) system.
    Example: Many drugs are metabolized by liver enzymes. If a patient is taking two drugs that both use the same enzyme (e.g., statins and certain antifungals), one drug's levels might increase because the enzyme is "busy" with the other, leading to increased side effects.
  • Excretion: The removal of the drug and its metabolites from the body, primarily through the kidneys (urine) or liver (bile/feces).
    Example: Patients with kidney impairment might need lower doses of renally excreted drugs (like many antibiotics) to prevent accumulation and toxicity.

Mechanism of Action (MOA) for Common Drug Classes

Mechanism of Action describes *how* a drug produces its effect in the body. Understanding MOA helps in predicting therapeutic effects and side effects.

  • Statins (e.g., Atorvastatin, Simvastatin):

    MOA: Inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, an enzyme crucial for cholesterol synthesis in the liver. This leads to decreased cholesterol production and increased LDL receptor activity, which removes more LDL ("bad") cholesterol from the blood.

    Indication: Hyperlipidemia (high cholesterol) to reduce the risk of cardiovascular events.
  • ACE Inhibitors (e.g., Lisinopril, Enalapril):

    MOA: Block the enzyme Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme (ACE), preventing the conversion of Angiotensin I to Angiotensin II. Angiotensin II is a potent vasoconstrictor and stimulates aldosterone release. By blocking its formation, ACE inhibitors cause vasodilation (widening of blood vessels) and reduce sodium/water retention, lowering blood pressure.

    Indication: Hypertension (high blood pressure), heart failure, and post-myocardial infarction.
  • Beta-Blockers (e.g., Metoprolol, Carvedilol):

    MOA: Block beta-adrenergic receptors in the heart and blood vessels. This reduces heart rate, contractility, and blood vessel constriction, leading to lower blood pressure and reduced workload on the heart.

    Indication: Hypertension, angina (chest pain), heart failure, arrhythmias, and anxiety.
  • SSRI Antidepressants (e.g., Sertraline, Escitalopram):

    MOA: Selectively inhibit the reuptake of serotonin in the brain, increasing the amount of serotonin available in the synaptic cleft. Serotonin is a neurotransmitter involved in mood regulation, sleep, and appetite.

    Indication: Depression, anxiety disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), and panic disorder.
  • Antibiotics (General):

    MOA: Vary widely depending on the class, but generally work by:

    • Inhibiting bacterial cell wall synthesis (e.g., Penicillins, Cephalosporins).
    • Inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis (e.g., Macrolides, Tetracyclines).
    • Inhibiting bacterial DNA replication or repair (e.g., Fluoroquinolones).
    • Disrupting bacterial cell membrane (e.g., Polymyxins).

    Indication: Bacterial infections. Specific bacteria are susceptible to specific antibiotic classes.

Common Dosage Forms and Routes of Administration

Medications come in various forms and can be administered through different routes. Understanding these is key to safe and effective dispensing.

Dosage Forms

  • Tablet: Solid, compressed powder. Can be swallowed whole, chewed, or dissolved. (e.g., Lisinopril tablet, chewable Aspirin)
  • Capsule: Medication enclosed in a soluble gelatin shell. (e.g., Amoxicillin capsule, Duloxetine capsule)
  • Solution: A liquid preparation where the drug is completely dissolved in a solvent. Appears clear. (e.g., Oral solutions, IV solutions like Normal Saline)
  • Suspension: A liquid preparation containing undissolved drug particles dispersed in a liquid vehicle. Requires shaking before use. (e.g., Amoxicillin suspension, many liquid antacids)
  • Elixir: A clear, sweet, hydroalcoholic liquid used for oral administration. (e.g., Digoxin elixir)
  • Syrup: A concentrated aqueous solution of sugar or a sugar substitute with a medication. (e.g., Cough syrups)
  • Cream: A semi-solid emulsion of oil and water, often for topical application, less greasy than ointments. (e.g., Hydrocortisone cream)
  • Ointment: A greasy, semi-solid preparation for topical application, providing a protective and occlusive layer. (e.g., Bacitracin ointment)
  • Gel: A semi-solid system consisting of a liquid phase within a 3D polymeric matrix. (e.g., Diclofenac gel)
  • Patch (Transdermal): A medicated adhesive patch placed on the skin to deliver a specific dose of medication through the skin and into the bloodstream. (e.g., Nicotine patch, Fentanyl patch)
  • Suppository: A solid dosage form inserted into a body orifice (rectum, vagina, urethra) where it dissolves or melts to release medication. (e.g., Promethazine suppository)
  • Inhaler (Aerosol/MDI/DPI): Devices that deliver medication directly to the lungs. (e.g., Albuterol inhaler)
  • Injection (Vial/Ampule/Prefilled Syringe): Liquid medication administered via needle into a vein (IV), muscle (IM), or under the skin (SQ).

Routes of Administration

  • Oral (PO - per os): Taken by mouth and swallowed. Most common and convenient.
  • Topical: Applied to the skin surface for local effect. (e.g., creams, ointments)
  • Transdermal: Applied to the skin for systemic absorption through the skin. (e.g., patches)
  • Parenteral: Administration by injection, bypassing the gastrointestinal tract. Includes:
    • Intravenous (IV): Into a vein. Fastest onset of action.
    • Intramuscular (IM): Into a muscle.
    • Subcutaneous (SQ/SC): Under the skin.
    • Intradermal (ID): Into the dermis layer of the skin.
  • Inhalation: Breathed into the lungs. (e.g., inhalers, nebulizers)
  • Rectal: Inserted into the rectum. (e.g., suppositories, enemas)
  • Vaginal: Inserted into the vagina. (e.g., creams, suppositories)
  • Ophthalmic: Applied to the eye. (e.g., eye drops, eye ointments)
  • Otic: Applied to the ear. (e.g., ear drops)
  • Nasal: Administered through the nose. (e.g., nasal sprays)
